Is The More Expensive Always Better?

Introduction

This project explores how price affects brand rank and satisfaction.

Does the price difference between brands influence customer satisfaction rankings?
In this study, we analyzed data by looking at the price and customer ranking to understand how pricing affects consumer perceptions and overall satisfaction.

Research Hypotheses

We start with hypothesis testing and move on to regression analysis.

H1: Higher-priced cosmetic products tend to receive higher customer satisfaction rankings.
H2: The relationship between price and satisfaction differs between luxury and non-luxury brands.
H3: Skin type suitability moderates the relationship between price and satisfaction.
H4: Even after controlling for brand category and skin type, price remains a significant predictor of satisfaction.

Hypothesis Statement

Hypothesis Statement

Null Hypothesis (H₀): There is no difference in actual quality between expensive and less expensive products.
μ₁ = μ₂
Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): Expensive products are perceived or rated as significantly better in quality than less expensive products.
μ₁ > μ₂

Dataset Description

This project is based on a dataset of 1472 cosmetic items with their price, ranking, and division into luxury and non-luxury brands. The goal is to explore whether price influences satisfaction as measured by product rankings.

The dataset contains:

We intentionally balanced the dataset to include both premium and budget segments for a fair comparison.

Statistical Variable Definitions (System Map A)

μ (mu): Average Satisfaction

Definition: Mean consumer satisfaction within a specific price range.

Known: Rating scale (1–5), price range, analysis period

Unknown: Population mean satisfaction score

Example: Average rating for products priced $50–$100.

σ² (sigma squared): Rating Variance

Definition: Variance in satisfaction ratings among consumers in the same price group.

Known: Rating scale (1–5), sample size, analysis period

Unknown: Population variance

Example: Low-priced products with ratings from 3 to 5 stars.

p: Proportion of Satisfied Users

Definition: Share of users rating ≥ 4 (defined as "satisfied").

Known: Satisfaction threshold, price range, analysis period

Unknown: True proportion of satisfied consumers

Example: 78% of luxury product users rated 4 or 5 stars.

β (beta): Brand/Category Effect

Definition: The influence of product category or brand type on satisfaction scores.

Known: Brand type (e.g. luxury), category (e.g. skincare)

Unknown: Actual effect on consumer ratings

Example: Skincare rated 0.2 points higher on average than makeup.

γ (gamma): Skin Type Suitability

Definition: Effect of product suitability for normal skin on satisfaction.

Known: Suitability for normal skin (0 or 1), analysis duration

Unknown: How much satisfaction varies by suitability

Example: Products for normal skin scored 0.3 higher on average.

Regression Models: Understanding Price & Satisfaction

We tested four regression models to analyze how price affects satisfaction.

Model 1: Original Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price This baseline model represents the total effect of product price on satisfaction. A positive β₁ (β₁ > 0) indicates that higher-priced items tend to receive higher satisfaction rankings. However, since this model does not control for other variables (e.g., brand, category), β₁ may capture both the true effect of price and hidden confounding influences.

Confounders And Moderators

Model 2: Confounder Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price + β₂·Category Category may influence both Price and Ranking. Including this variable removes potential bias in β₁. The difference in β₁ before and after adding Category indicates the confounding effect.

Model 3: Comfounder Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price + β₂·BrandType + β₃·(Price × BrandType) Brand Type acts as a comfounder in the relationship between Price and Ranking. If β₃ > 0, the effect of Price is stronger for luxury brands. This interaction term reveals conditional effects that depend on brand category.

Model 4: Moderator Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price + β₂·Normal Normal acts as a moderator in the relationship between Price and Ranking. This model means that this product is not for specific skin types. This model isolates the direct effect of Price. A substantial drop in β₁ compared to the original model suggests an indirect effect via Normal.

# Model 3: Confounder - Interaction with BrandType

# Model 4: Moderator - Controlling for Normal
model4 = smf.ols('Ranking ~ Price + Normal', data=df).fit()

These regression models were estimated using Python's Statsmodels library. Each model provides a different perspective on how Price influences Satisfaction, depending on whether confounding, moderating, or mediating variables are included.

Hidden Variables (System Map B)

System Map B introduces several latent (unobserved) variables that were not explicitly included in System Map A but significantly influence observed relationships. These variables—Quality, Size, and Advertising—affect both independent and dependent variables such as Price and Satisfaction, potentially introducing bias if left uncontrolled.

1. Quality (Confounder)

Definition: The intrinsic product standard that directly influences both price and customer satisfaction.

Confounding Role: Higher quality often justifies higher prices and also enhances perceived satisfaction, thus potentially inflating the observed effect of price on satisfaction.

Measurement Challenge: Difficult to quantify from rating data alone; typically inferred from expert reviews or long-term brand reputation.

2. Size (Volume)

Definition: The physical quantity or volume of the product.

Effect Pathway: Directly affects product price even within the same category. Larger sizes often come at higher prices, skewing simple price-based comparisons.

Implication: Without accounting for volume, price alone may be a misleading proxy for value or quality.

3. Advertising (Latent Influencer)

Definition: Promotional efforts undertaken by brands to influence customer perception and behavior.

Multifaceted Impact:

  • Brand Strategy: Different companies invest differently in advertising intensity and tone.
  • Marketing Budget: Spending levels directly affect product visibility and consumer trust.
  • Celebrity Endorsements: Shape customer expectations and brand image, thereby indirectly influencing satisfaction scores.
  • Cost Recovery: High advertising expenditure is often passed on to consumers via increased prices.

Data Limitation: Advertising effort is typically not captured in product review datasets, making it a critical hidden driver of perceived value.

Social Impact

1.Consumer Protection

Many people believe expensive products are better, but this isn’t always true. High prices often come from packaging or ads not real quality. For example, a $30 cream might work just as well as a $120 one. If customers don’t get clear information, they can waste money. That’s why honest labeling is important (OECD, 2022).

2.Market Fairness

Small brands often can’t afford fancy ads or designs, even if their quality is great. This can lead to low ratings and poor sales just because they “look cheap.” For example, a good local product might be ignored next to a luxury brand. This hurts small businesses and limits customer choices. Fair marketing helps everyone compete.
