Is The More Expensive Always Better?

Introduction

This project explores how price affects brand rank and satisfaction.

Does the price difference between brands influence customer satisfaction rankings?
In this study, we analyzed data by looking at the price and customer ranking to understand how pricing affects consumer perceptions and overall satisfaction.

Research Hypotheses

We start with hypothesis testing and move on to regression analysis.

H1: Higher-priced cosmetic products tend to receive higher customer satisfaction rankings.
H2: The relationship between price and satisfaction differs between luxury and non-luxury brands.
H3: Skin type suitability moderates the relationship between price and satisfaction.
H4: Even after controlling for brand category and skin type, price remains a significant predictor of satisfaction.

Dataset Description

This project is based on a dataset of 1472 cosmetic items with their price, ranking, and division into luxury and non-luxury brands. The goal is to explore whether price influences satisfaction as measured by product rankings.

The dataset contains:

Variable Description
NameProduct name or label (e.g., Crème de, Facial Treatment)
BrandCosmetic brand (e.g., LA MER, SK-II)
PriceRetail price in USD
RankUser rating score (1 to 5)
Skin Type0/1 variables for Dry, Normal, Oily, Sensitive
Brand Type"Luxury" or "Non-luxury"

We intentionally balanced the dataset to include both premium and budget segments for a fair comparison.

Statistical Variable Definitions (System Map A)

μ (mu): Average Satisfaction

Definition: The average consumer satisfaction rating for products within a specific price range.

Known: Rating scale (1–5), price range, analysis period

Unknown: Population mean satisfaction score

Example: The average review rating for products priced between $50 and $100.

σ² (sigma squared): Rating Variance

Definition: The variance of satisfaction ratings (1–5 stars) among consumers in the same price range.

Known: Rating scale (1–5), sample size, analysis duration

Unknown: Population variance of satisfaction ratings

Example: Low-priced products showing a wide spread of ratings from 3 to 5 stars.

p: Proportion of Satisfied Users

Definition: The proportion of consumers considered "satisfied" (rating ≥ 4).

Known: Satisfaction threshold (≥4), price range, analysis period

Unknown: True population proportion of satisfied users

Example: 78% of luxury brand users gave ratings of 4 or higher.

β (beta): Category or Brand Effect

Definition: The effect size of product category or brand type on satisfaction.

Known: Brand type (luxury vs non-luxury), category (e.g., skincare vs makeup)

Unknown: The actual impact of brand or category on satisfaction

Example: Skincare products rated 0.2 points higher on average than makeup products.

γ (gamma): Skin Type Suitability

Definition: The impact of skin type suitability (Normal) on satisfaction.

Known: Whether a product suits Normal skin (0/1), analysis duration

Unknown: How much satisfaction differs for users with Normal skin

Example: Products suitable for Normal skin scored 0.3 points higher on average.

Regression Models: Understanding Price & Satisfaction

We tested four regression models to analyze how price affects satisfaction.

1. Original Model

Equation: Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price

This model shows the total effect of price on satisfaction. If β₁ > 0, then higher-priced items tend to get higher rankings.

Confounders And Moderators

Model 2: Confounder Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price + β₂·Category Category may influence both Price and Ranking. Including this variable removes potential bias in β₁. The difference in β₁ before and after adding Category indicates the confounding effect.

Model 3: Comfounder Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price + β₂·BrandType + β₃·(Price × BrandType) Brand Type acts as a comfounder in the relationship between Price and Ranking. If β₃ > 0, the effect of Price is stronger for luxury brands. This interaction term reveals conditional effects that depend on brand category.

Model 4: Moderator Model

Equation Interpretation
Ranking = β₀ + β₁·Price + β₂·Normal Normal acts as a moderator in the relationship between Price and Ranking. This model means that this product is not for specific skin types. This model isolates the direct effect of Price. A substantial drop in β₁ compared to the original model suggests an indirect effect via Normal.

Hidden Variables

System Map B includes several hidden variables not identified in System Map A. These hidden variables—quality, size, and advertising—play a crucial role in influencing other factors such as price and satisfaction.

Quality

Quality is considered a hidden variable because it cannot be easily quantified. It acts as a confounder, affecting both price and customer satisfaction. Higher quality often leads to higher prices and greater satisfaction.

Size (Volume)

Size or volume influences price directly. Even for products of equal type or category, different sizes can result in different pricing structures, making size a key hidden factor.

Advertising

Advertising is another hidden variable not shown in the dataset, yet influenced by various factors:

  • It depends on the brand, as each company follows a unique advertising strategy.
  • The amount spent on commercials varies across companies.
  • Celebrity endorsements in ads can influence customer satisfaction by shaping brand perception.
  • High advertising costs often lead to higher product prices to recover marketing expenses.

Social Impact

1.Consumer Protection

Many people believe expensive products are better, but this isn’t always true. High prices often come from packaging or ads not real quality. For example, a $30 cream might work just as well as a $120 one. If customers don’t get clear information, they can waste money. That’s why honest labeling is important (OECD, 2022).

2.Market Fairness

Small brands often can’t afford fancy ads or designs, even if their quality is great. This can lead to low ratings and poor sales just because they “look cheap.” For example, a good local product might be ignored next to a luxury brand. This hurts small businesses and limits customer choices. Fair marketing helps everyone compete.
